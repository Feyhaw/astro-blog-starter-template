title: Encapsulating the natural state 
subtitle: World engineering at Biosphere 2 Laboratory

description: Architectural examination of Biosphere 2 as a capsule architecture

abstract: | 
    The Biosphere 2 laboratory was a materially closed artificial biosphere that first entered operation in 1991. In addition to its controversies, it was known for its unprecedented scales, space rhetoric, and promises of scientific discovery. The media coverage has mainly portrayed the Biosphere 2 laboratory as a research institution since its early operations, yet this perspective captures only one of its many roles. Regrettably, however, its cultural and theoretical ties with the Countercultural Movement are less often visited. Most importantly, the architectural analysis of the structure remains marginal. While providing an overview of the Biosphere 2 laboratory’s history, this text examines the design from multiple perspectives, including architecture, science, culture, and finance. I argue that Biosphere 2’s controversies are deeply rooted in its struggle with multiple missions which sometimes conflict with one another. Furthermore, Biosphere 2's struggles stem from a clash between different cultures, represented by the subjective consciousness promoted during the Countercultural Movement and the rigid, rational reasoning of science, finance, and engineering. Through reviewing the materials from different fields and perspectives, it was revealed that the conflicts involved in the facility’s design and operation are manifestations of this clash. This text is by no means aiming to determine whether Biosphere 2 is a success or failure; it is an architectural presentation of the site conditions that made the project possible yet controversial in later years.
    
    Biosphere 2 remains referential today for architectural studies and design. As a capsule architecture, Biosphere 2 demonstrated the creation and maintenance of a world analogue. Biosphere 2’s analogue is not a perfect one, but its imperfections provide an opportunity to study the designs. Like other capsule architectures, such as those in the Arctic and Antarctic, Biosphere 2 allows us to observe the attempt to define and channel the natural state, which is perhaps a constant theme of architectural exploration but usually obscured by other vocabularies and concepts. On the theoretical level, Biosphere 2 provides a mode for analysing the human-technology-nature relationship in architecture and engineering. At a practical level, Biosphere 2’s experience can be applied in the future design of closed ecosystems. With climate change and the new Space Race in sight, longer stays in extreme isolation and remoteness, such as the polar regions and space, can be expected.
