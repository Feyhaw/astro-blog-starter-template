title: Living the Unlivable 
subtitle: Extreme Architectures in Polar and Space Exploration

description: Historical and theoretical review of space and polar architectures

abstract: | 
    When an architecture is designed in our neighbourhood, it is likely to be regarded as a daily part of life, no matter who inhabits it. In other words, it was imagined as a way of life, and that imagination coalesces into strokes of lines, curves, and dots – symbols that suggest a better picture of the world. This is very much true across borders and throughout time, from primitive huts to Polynesian shrines. The title is therefore interpreted as architecture in extreme environments, where extreme survival conditions for humans make architecture special, rather than as an attribution of exceptionality to architecture. It is a way of adaptation. There are many extreme environments humans have encountered so far; only three will be focused on in this research: the Arctic, Antarctic, and outer space. Coldness and scarcity dominate the zone of death, and these domains are continually haunted by similar political and cultural narrations.
    
    Since the 1950s, humans have devoted tremendous effort to exploring these formerly impenetrable places and striving to extend the duration of their stay. The architectures that made the mission possible have been showing a trend of resemblance in terms of form and function: not only are they becoming similar within the same domain, but also across different domains. How site conditions shape this development is one question, and why humans build architecture in those places is quite another. Given the brief history and a small number of precedents, the architectures from the three extreme environments are perhaps more important for what they open up. They are often characterised by enterprise spirit, colonial sentiments, scientific curiosity, and so on. By carefully isolating perceptions from the architectures themselves, this research aims to unveil the cultural and political connections, beyond similar physical conditions, behind the resembling architectural typologies.
    
    There has been considerable scholarship on the architectures in the aforementioned domains. There are, however, few juxtapositions of those architectures in terms of their history, development, and regulations. In particular, the comparative cultural and political analysis of those architectures since the early Cold War has remained under the radar. As a history-theory researcher, I wish to trace the history of human exploration in the three realms. Topics such as the Cold War, the Space Race, and cultural movements will be discussed alongside architectural development, as they often provide valuable insights and different perspectives. Site conditions will be studied and compared. Furthermore, technological transfer within and across the domains will be examined through the lenses of political conflicts and cultural constructs.
