The history of Arctic and Antarctic explorations is by no means confined to the modern era, yet sustained habitation in these challenging environments did not occur until the postwar years. This paper focuses on the 1950s and one pivotal event: the 1957 International Geophysical Year, when technological and social developments in polar architecture matured and took concrete form. Drawing on George Kennan’s concept of “containment,” it explores the social, cultural, and ideological dimensions of contain-ment as they were inscribed in polar architecture. Case studies are organized according to their respective “scales” of containment. Although polar and space architecture have received considerable academic attention, their connections to contemporaneous archi-tectures “back home” are often overlooked. Moreover, existing studies tend to remain within engineering or ergonomics, emphasizing technological concerns while neglect-ing systemic architectural perspectives. This article is part a wider research effort that seeks to bridge this gap. By tracing polar architectures from the smallest to the largest scale, the article shows how early Cold War containment was enacted across multiple levels and through diverse means. Produced under the same historical conditions as other contemporary projects—and marked by the same anxieties and ambitions—polar architectures offer a distinctive lens through which to study the layering of contain-ment. They provided not only shelter and connectivity but also ideological security and cultural integrity, functioning as tools of social programming.
