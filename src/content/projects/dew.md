From a humble origin in the corn fields of Illinois, the Distant Early Warning, or DEW Line, system reshaped North America. Initially conceptualized shortly after the Second World War, the radar system was designed to provide early warning, buying precious three to six hours for cities and military to respond before nuclear-armed Soviet bombers eventually show up in front of the doorstep. In total of 67 radar stations stretched from Alaska, across vast Canadian Arctic, to ice cape of Greenland. Dedicated for the full-time surveillance over the Arctic air space, the DEW Line integrated various technologies such as housing, communication, and computation. It was hailed as a great engineering feat, satisfying the American public with yet another technological sublime. 

Although the system was soon proven obsolete by the Soviet rockets, its influence remained profound: the continent of North America was redefined as a political concept, the “internet” network was first pioneered, a national system of interstate highway was built, creating the bipolar urban structure of high-rise downtown office towers and vast suburbs. From today’s perspective, the DEW Line system seems no more than an air traffic control system; however, without which, the Arctic might remain largely unknown to the Canadian people who claim its very sovereignty; Inuit culture might largely remain in its original state of isolation; and Buckminster Fuller might remain largely “nobody.”

This article investigates the DEW Line system: its history, technology, socio-political background, and its impact. The DEW Line will serve as the primary case study in this text; the greater background of the SAGE system and interstate highways will also be introduced. Towards the end of the text, the geodesic dome will be discussed with the background of surveillance and communication. As one of the earliest modern polar architectures, the DEW Line will be analysed from the perspective of cybernetics: its concept and practices. Among the many long-standing influences the DEW Line has had on modern society, I will focus on the mechanisms of confinement and control inscribed into its infrastructural systems. The DEW is the first infrastructure project that was designed to have humans substituted by machines. The role of artificial intelligence in polar and space exploration will be briefly discussed. This text will lay the foundation for next investigation, which focuses on how certain architectural designs facilitate social programming and human engineering.
