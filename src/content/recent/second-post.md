<h3>AirconF Experiment Report</h3>

<p>
<strong>Background</strong>
</p>

<p>
The 2026 heatwaves in Europe are phenomenal. The earliest one hit at the end of May, reaching a record of 33 Celsius. On the other hand, England, a Northwest European region, was not traditionally demanding of air conditioners, leading to unpreparedness for this series of hot weather.
</p>

<p>
On the other hand, the cheapest portable air-conditioner costs around 800 pounds. In addition to the high cost of electricity, it will be used for only a very short period each year. Given that the hottest days roughly add up to one month in England, the air-conditioner will be left unused for the rest of the time, possibly leading to additional costs for maintenance.
</p>

<p>
Therefore, a semi-air-conditioned device was conceived on my trip to London to meet my supervisors on May 22. As an architecture PhD candidate who has long been exposed not only to the practices of digital and physical modelling, but also to calculations related to the thermal envelope, I wish to build an ice-based cooling device that can reduce the temperature by at least 5 degrees Celsius.
</p>

<p>
Such a device is certainly not the first I have invented. Ice has been used as a traditional cooling medium in many cultures, and people have mastered ways to preserve or even manufacture it in summer before the invention of physical-chemical air conditioners in the 20th century. This is not my first attempt to build a homemade air-conditioner either. In 2020, during the pandemic lockdown in Toronto, I was motivated to develop such a device in my bedroom. I fixed the water pipe on the back of an electric fan and hoped that the circulation of iced water inside the pipe would cool the air as it passed through the fan. To make sure the ice water stayed cool, I put the entire device inside the closet. The experiment failed, as no observable decrease in temperature was observed.
<p>

<p>
It should be noted that the attempt was not entirely a failure. Several lessons were learned, such as the need for a thermal envelope to prevent the ice water from warming too quickly. Also, my landlord was deeply impressed by the effort – I guess – as he bought me a portable air conditioner the next day. 
<p><br>

<p>
<strong>Experiment Design</strong>
</p>

<p>
The device consists of four main components: a thermally resistant container, a cake bag [1], a continuous wind channel, and electric fans. The principle is simple: fans introduce flowing air into the air channel; after it is cooled in the main cooling chamber, it flows out from the other end of the system. The cost to build such a device, if not taking into account human labour, can be kept within 50 pounds.
</p>

<p>
The challenge in designing the system mainly lies in the thermal design. AirconF features a multi-stage cooling mechanism with pre-cooling stages. Before the air enters the main cooling chamber, it is cooled by the ice-filled container through thermal conduction. After the cooled air leaves the main cooling chamber, it is not immediately exposed to the environment; instead, it passes through the cavity between the container and the cake bag before being blown out of the system. In such a way, any direct exposure of the ice and the environment is prevented, therefore allowing the system to function for a longer time without resupply of the cooling agent.
</p>

<img src="/images/aircon F/concept.jpg">
<p>
The direction of air flow in the initial concept was opposite to the later model. 
<p><br>

<p>
There are two advantages in this design. Firstly, the system does not rely on direct contact between air and water (ice), resulting in drier output. Many products currently on the market rely on water evaporation to absorb energy from the air. Their prices are around the same range as my device; however, in theory, they do not function well on days with high relative humidity, which is often the case in the West Midlands. The excessive humidity could lead to a higher rate of rusting for my electronic devices; it is a risk I cannot afford to take. Second, the evaporation-based cooling fans don’t work in winter. One may wish to add hot water into the fan’s container, but the wind will instead absorb more heat from the air, leading to the undesirable cooling effect in the middle of January. Since my device prevents direct contact between water and air, it can still be used for heating in winter, and the principle remains the same.
</p>

<p>
The system's possible flaws involve the design of the air channel. To keep ice away from direct exposure to the environment, the air channels, both inward and outward, must be designed in a rather twisted way, which considerably hampers the continuity of the air current. The negative outcome could be a discontinuity in airflow throughout the system, where warm air couldn’t get in and cooled air couldn’t get out. If the fans are not powerful enough to create a significant pressure difference, the system would eventually collapse into a larger beer box. As the later experiments show, the fans play a delicate role in the system.
<p><br>

<p>
<strong>Mark I</strong>
</p>

<p>
Mark I was assembled on June 21. The connecting agent was hot glue, something I learned to play around with back in undergrad. It was first tested on Jun 23, and the temperature measured at the exhaust was 3 degrees Celsius lower than the ambient temperature. It worked, but the wind was weak, and the effect was too small to register in bodily sensation. The fans come with 3 rpm levels, but the specific numbers were not disclosed in the manual. The fan ratios between the out-fan and in-fan were kept at 2:2 for this experiment, meaning level 2 at the intake and level 2 at the exhaust.
</p>

<p>
In general, since the wind was weak anyway, there is no point in comparing the performance at different fan ratios. If they are level 3, it will be too noisy. If they are level 1, there is barely any wind at all. Therefore, the twisted tunnel was thought to be the main cause of the issue. It will be addressed in further modifications.
</p><br>

<p>
<strong>Mark II</strong>
</p>

<p>
AirconF was modified on July 2. The original twisty air channel was replaced with a straight in-and-out wind tunnel, powered by fans on the opposite sides. The wind tunnel was built with aluminum cans (from the Monster energy drink) for better thermal conductivity. On the bottom-facing side of the central segment of the wind tunnel, holes were poked to allow direct heat exchange between the warm air and cooled air. My hypothesis is that some cooled air will be sucked into the wind tunnel due to the Bernoulli effect: the relatively higher air velocity inside the wind tunnel will cause a relatively lower air pressure. In addition, the lower cavity between the central container and the cake bag was preserved to allow replenish of the air inside the main cooling chamber.
</p>

<p>
The first experiment was done on July 3. The biggest temperature difference ever confirmed was observed on this day. The fan ratio was kept 2:2, as 3:3 would be unnecessarily too loud. The highlight of this experiment, however, is the July 8 experiment. It lasted 11 and a half hours and contributed many interesting discoveries, not to mention the performance record. But this time, the largest temperature difference the device produced was only 3 degrees Celsius.
<p><br>

<p>
<strong>Disussion</strong>
</p>

<p>
Since the device remains unchanged during the test, this section will explain the causality between the variable and the conditions open for adjustment.
</p>

<p>
<strong>1.</strong>The quantity of Ice<br>
The quantity makes a difference. With more ice, the temperature inside the cooling chamber can be further lowered. The final temperature of two tests with Mark I can reflect this relationship. The container can hold up to six bottles; therefore, the final stable temperature at the exhaust could be lower than 23 Celsius. When another bottle of iced water was added in the second test of Mark II at 02:02, the chamber temperature dropped, but the exhaust temperature increased. It is likely due to the slow re-cooling process around the wind tunnel, since the chamber was opened to add the item. In the first three experiments, after hours of operation, the ice water and blocks remained largely frozen – it is a bad sign. As a cooling agent, it is supposed to absorb the heat from the air. If it melts too quickly, the thermal envelope is not working. If it melts too slowly, it means the heat in the air is merely absorbed, and the whole device fails.
<p>

<p>
<strong>2.</strong>The fan ratio<br>
Comparing fan ratios with two Mark I tests is meaningless, since the wind is weak anyway. For Mark II, the fan ratio makes a much more noticeable difference: due to the straight air tunnel, the feedback is almost immediate. The first test with Mark II lowered the temperature by 5°C, but this was not reproduced in the second test. The wind is slightly stronger than Mark I, but still not very effective. Note that in the second test of Mark II, the changes in fan ratio at 22:22 and 02:18 caused the chamber and exhaust temperatures to change. When switched to 2:2, the airflow through the wind tunnel was at a relatively constant velocity and was cooled by both the cooling chamber and the cooled air. However, when the fan ratio was switched back to 3:1, which means the out-fan worked at a higher rate than the in-fan, the chamber temperature increased while the exhaust temperature decreased. To compensate for the low pressure caused by the different fan ratio, more cooled air flowed into the wind tunnel from the cooling chamber, while ambient air flowed into the cooling chamber through the cavity. 
<p>

<p>
It should be noted that although I tried my best to replicate the conditions and maintain controlled conditions, every experiment has its own small differences, such as the location of the thermometer, which may have caused some degree of issue in reproducing the results. The noise is another drawback. Although I don’t have a device to measure the loudness of the sound it produces, it definitely causes an observable interruption during work. During sleep, however, I would say it can be some fine yet unintended white noise.
</p><br>

<p>
<strong>Conclusion</strong>
<p>

<p>
As a homemade air conditioner, AirconF can produce a temperature difference of 3 Celsius between the environment and its exhaust. The cooling effect is dramatic, if not psychological. The main issue is the fan's low power. With a higher rpm of the out-fan, the device may produce some cooler wind. Further experiments with more powerful fans (like desktop fans) can be expected if the heatwave continues. In general, I will not say it is a serious experiment or pretend to be some sort of authority on homemade air conditioners; it is simply an interesting activity and a good learning experience.
</p>

<p>
Initially, I never thought of writing this report or taking any recordings. But the creation of Capsule Fey has greatly motivated me to explore the potential of every undertaking. It reminds me of the many great inventors who lived on this land. England is a land of inventors and explorers, and the home of the Industrial Revolution. Although I’m not intending to be compared with great inventors of the past, this experiment, despite the unfulfilling goals, does make me feel closer to this country and its culture and tradition – of that I am deeply honoured.
</p>

<p>
Moreover, it should be noted that in the current economic and climate environment, personal discomfort during the heatwave may reflect a larger-scale failure. It is time to take climate change seriously and stop wasting money on wars. With AirconF, I devote my mockery.
</p>
